I
§ 01The Premise

Treasuries are the safest yield on earth — but the rails are stuck in 1975.

The asset is excellent. The infrastructure around it — bank holidays, T+2, accreditation gates, opaque custody — was designed for a world that no longer exists.

What Wall Street still ships

Settlement, 1975 vintage

  1. Markets close at 4 p.m. ET. Treasuries do not trade nights, weekends, or holidays.
  2. T+1 / T+2 settlement. Capital is locked in transit while paperwork clears.
  3. Account minimums, accreditation gates, and brokers stand between most of the world and a 5% risk-free rate.
  4. Holdings sit in custodial silos — they cannot be programmed, composed, or pledged across systems.
  5. Cross-border distribution requires a chain of correspondent banks, each adding fee and delay.
The Ondo Thesis

Same yield, new rails

  1. Tokens trade 24/7/365 on public blockchains, with daily yield accrual built into the contract.
  2. Mint and redeem are instant against USDC at the smart-contract level, with no broker in the middle.
  3. $5,000 minimum, KYC/AML at issuance, then free transfer between approved holders.
  4. Tokens are composable: collateral in DeFi, settlement on cross-border rails, treasury reserves for DAOs.
  5. Issuance is regulator-aware — Reg D / Reg S / 3(c)(7) wrappers, with custody at BlackRock, Coinbase, Fireblocks.

A money-market fund as an ERC-20 — and the boring magic is that nothing about the underlying changes.

§ 02Product Stack

Five products, one thesis: bring institutional finance on-chain without breaking it.

U.S. Dollar Yield Token · Non-U.S.

USDY

— a productive dollar —

A yield-bearing token backed by short-term U.S. Treasuries and bank demand deposits, structured for non-U.S. retail and institutional holders. Legally, USDY is interest-bearing tokenized debt — not a stablecoin — so the price drifts upward as Treasury interest accrues into the underlying portfolio.

USDY ships in two flavours: USDY (accumulating — price rises) and rUSDY (rebasing — balance grows daily, price stays at $1). The wrapper contract converts between them on demand.

Note · 01
Architect's Note

A "yield stablecoin" in everything but legal classification

USDY is the closest the market has come to a productive dollar — it behaves like USDC but accrues T-bill yield natively. For a bank treasury exploring tokenized cash, USDY is the reference design: collateral-eligible, transfer-restricted, audited, and with a clean legal wrapper (Reg S debt instrument) that maps to existing fixed-income desks. The 40-day restricted period mirrors a Reg S distribution compliance period, not a technical limitation.

Ondo Short-Term U.S. Gov. Treasuries · Qualified U.S.

OUSG

— a wrapper around the wrappers —

A tokenized fund providing exposure to short-term U.S. Treasuries through institutional money-market vehicles — primarily BlackRock's BUIDL, alongside Franklin Templeton's BENJI, Fidelity, WisdomTree, and Wellington. OUSG is the gold standard for U.S.-qualified on-chain treasury management.

Like USDY, OUSG comes in accumulating and rebasing forms (OUSG / rOUSG). Mint and redeem are instant via USDC or PYUSD, 24/7, against the daily-published Net Asset Value.

Note · 02
Architect's Note

A wrapper around the wrappers

OUSG is technically a fund-of-funds: it holds shares in BUIDL (which itself holds Treasuries), plus USDC and bank deposits as a liquidity buffer to honour 24/7 redemptions. The clever part is the NAV oracle — the smart contract publishes a per-token NAV daily, and mint/redeem math is just USDC ÷ NAV. No order book, no slippage, no counterparty matching. For a bank balance sheet, this is "money market fund as ERC-20."

Note · 03
Architect's Note

Where the regulatory moat lives

OGM only became viable after Ondo acquired Oasis Pro Markets — an SEC-registered broker-dealer, ATS, and transfer agent. The architecture is "tokens minted against custodied positions held by a regulated broker." For banks evaluating tokenized securities issuance, the takeaway is that the technical primitives are now commodity; the moat is the broker-dealer license, the Reg ATS approval, and the transfer-agent appointment that allows direct on-chain registration of beneficial ownership.

DeFi Lending Protocol · Compound v2 Fork

Flux Finance

— borrow without unwinding —

A peer-to-pool lending protocol governed by the Ondo DAO and built on a fork of Compound v2. Flux's distinguishing feature: it supports permissioned RWA collateral (OUSG, USDY) alongside open-access assets like USDC, DAI, FRAX, and USDT — letting Treasury holders borrow stablecoins without unwinding their yield position.

For institutional users this is capital efficiency unavailable in TradFi: earn ~5% on Treasuries while simultaneously using them as collateral to borrow operating capital at the prevailing DeFi rate.

Note · 04
Architect's Note

The first lending protocol that respects KYC at the contract level

Flux solved a problem most DeFi forks hand-wave: how do you list a transfer-restricted token in an open lending pool? The answer is per-asset eligibility checks at the cToken level, allow-listed against the same registry that gates USDY transfers. This is the design pattern banks should copy when extending an existing lending stack to RWAs — keep the AMM/pool math, swap the transfer hook.

L1 Blockchain · Cosmos SDK + EVM · Live Nov 2025

Ondo Chain

— compliance at the consensus layer —

A purpose-built Layer-1 designed around a single conviction: tokenized real-world assets need infrastructure that is institutionally compliant by default, not bolted on. Built on the Cosmos SDK with EVM compatibility (CometBFT consensus, sub-second finality) — keeping every Solidity contract and Foundry tool that Ethereum developers already know.

The novel parts live below the application layer: enshrined oracles built into consensus, on-chain proof of reserves, permissioned validators drawn from regulated financial institutions, and RWA-backed staking where validators secure the network with tokenized Treasuries instead of a volatile native token.

Note · 05
Architect's Note

A permissioned validator set on a public chain — and that is the point.

Ondo Chain is the first credible answer to "what would a bank-run Layer-1 actually look like?" The validator set is closed (so no MEV from anonymous parties, no front-running of tokenized equity orders), but the chain itself is public and the data is open. RWA-backed staking is the most interesting primitive here — it removes the speculative-token tail-risk from validator economics. For KBTG-grade infrastructure planning, this is the design language to study, even if the chain you ship is private.

VIII
§ 08What Can Go Wrong

The honest list of risks an architect should price in.

i
Risk · 01

Custody & counterparty

Token holders ultimately depend on BlackRock, Coinbase, Fireblocks, and Zodia honouring their custodial obligations. A failure at any of these — operational, regulatory, or fraud — flows straight to the on-chain token. Diversification across custodians is the mitigation, but not elimination.

ii
Risk · 02

Regulatory drift

USDY relies on Reg S exemption; OUSG on Reg D 506(c) and 3(c)(7) status. The SEC closed its multi-year Ondo investigation in late 2025 without charges, and the CLARITY Act has provided structural clarity, but jurisdictional rules continue to evolve and could narrow distribution.

iii
Risk · 03

Smart contract & bridge risk

Ondo's contracts are audited and battle-tested, but bridges remain the highest-risk surface in crypto. The LayerZero-based Ondo Bridge concentrates trust in the decentralized verifier network; researchers tracked $14.6M in RWA exploits in 1H 2025 alone. Defence in depth still matters.
